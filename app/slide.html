A home to 200,000 agro-pastoralists is planned to be turned into an industrial powerhouse.

Ethiopia’s Lower Omo Valley, one of Africa’s most unique places and home to 200,000 agro-pastoralists is planned to be turned into an industrial powerhouse. The early stages of the development have resulted in a loss of land and livelihoods for several thousand of Ethiopia’s most vulnerable citizens and the future of 500,000 agro-pastoralists in Ethiopia and Kenya hang in the balance…

Lower Omo Valley

The Lower Omo Valley in southern Ethiopia is one of the most culturally and biologically diverse places on Earth. Approximately 200,000 indigenous people live in the Lower Omo Valley.

The Omo River flows south into Kenya’s Lake Turkana. Lake Turkana is the world’s largest desert lake and around 300,000 people live along its shores. The Omo and Turkana watershed is home to three national parks, and two World Heritage sites.

The Lower Omo Valley is also one of the most remote and underdeveloped regions of Ethiopia. But that is set to change

Zoom into the Valley

Tribal diversity

The Lower Omo Valley is home to many distinct indigenous peoples. All are intimately tied to the lands and waters around the Omo River for their livelihoods and cultural identity

Most people in this region are pastoralists. Most raise cattle, while some also grow sorghum along the Omo River and its tributaries while others harvest fish from the river.

Ethiopia’s sugar industry

Ethiopia produces 300,000 tons of sugar a year. With new sugar plantations in the Omo valley, the country aims to increase annual production to more than 2.25 million tons. This increase will be used to offset domestic sugar supply shortfalls, for ethanol production, and for export to sugar hungry markets. Exports of sugar and various biofuels have become very lucrative under the EU’s Everything but Arms treaty (EBA) which gives preferential trading terms to LDC nations such as Ethiopia.

Diversion dam

The construction of a diversion dam caught everyone by surprise when it was constructed in May 2012 to divert the waters of the Omo River into the sugar plantation’s irrigation canals. Not built to proper engineering standards the earthen structure broke twice before successfully diverting waters into the irrigation canals. Rising floodwaters behind this dam resulted in the flooding of Bodi farms, huts, and granaries. 220 households were displaced and their sorghum crops lost for the year. Impacts were also felt downstream where water levels dropped dramatically.

Villagization

According to government documents, all indigenous people in the Lower Omo Valley are to be resettled into permanent villages. These relocations are underway in the Bodi, Mursi, Nyangatom, and Hamer areas.As their traditional lands are being cleared for sugars, Bodi are being resettled into one of three permanent villages: Chunguro, Belolong, and Elegobia. The village locations are dry and most services are not yet available.

There are anecdotal reports that government is using food aid to lure people into the new villages. Many Bodi stay long enough to receive food aid and then leave the villages.In the Bodi areas, as of January 2013, an estimated 500 Bodi households had been resettled into the new villages.

Current impact

1200 Bodi living in the area of the reservoir have lost their homes and have had their livelihoods decimated as the floodwaters engulfed their farms and their grain stores. Some have moved into the new settlement villages whilst others are moving around with their cattle in the areas that have yet to be cleared for sugar.

Mursi and Kwegu families have also had their farms, beehives, and grain stores cleared.

The impacts are already devastating for those impacted individuals, but what is of particular concern is what will happen when the development scheme is fully implemented…

Current impact images

The future

Under the current proposals, all 200,000 of the Lower Omo’s indigenous people will be displaced from their ancestral lands as they will be relocated to permanent villages to provide labor to the sugar plantations.

They are concerned about the end of their way of life and the potential for inter-tribal conflict brought about by increased competition over what scarce grazing land will be left.

They have fears about how they will feed themselves and the social problems that will result from the rapid transition from agro-pastoralism to farm laborer.

There are also concerns in Kenya where projections show that water levels in Kenya’s Lake Turkana will drop by up to 22 meters, having devastating consequences on the livelihoods of the 300,000 Kenyans who rely on the lake for their livelihoods.

Summary

Some of Ethiopia's most vulnerable citizens are on the verge of losing their livelihoods and ability to feed themselves from Ethiopia's ambitious development plans in the Lower Omo Valley. Ethiopia must fulfill its obligations under international law and respect the rights of the indigenous peoples of the Lower Omo Valley to their traditional lands.

Indigenous Peoples

There is no agreed legal definition of ‘indigenous people’. However the UN’s Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues has approved the following description, stating that the UN ‘system’ has developed a modern understanding of this term [indigenous people] based on the following:

  • Self- identification as indigenous peoples at the individual level and accepted by the community as their member.
  • Historical continuity with pre-colonial and/or pre-settler societies
  • Strong link to territories and surrounding natural resources
  • Distinct social, economic or political systems.
  • Distinct language, culture and beliefs Form non-dominant groups of society
  • Resolve to maintain and reproduce their ancestral environments and systems as distinctive peoples and communities.

According to the UN the most fruitful approach is to identify, rather than define indigenous peoples. This is based on the fundamental criterion of self-identification as underlined in a number of human rights documents. UN Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, Factsheet: Who are Indigenous Peoples (Accessed 25 March 2012). The eight groups in the Lower Omo Valley fit this definition and are considered indigenous peoples.

Pastoralism

Definitions of pastoralism vary widely, but generally all involve the focus on livestock production and some form of mobility. Agro-pastoralism combines elements of pastoralism with sedentary agriculture. Many of the Lower Omo’s indigenous populations practice agro-pastoralism as they grow food along the banks of the Omo River and other fertile locations, in addition to cattle rearing. See http://data.iucn.org/wisp/pastoralism-definitions.html for more definitions of pastoralism.

“There will be big, state, commercial farms.”

Africa’s tallest dam has been under construction since 2006 and is scheduled to be in operation by 2013. It will generate 1870MW of much needed power for Ethiopia and neighboring countries, but at a cost to local people. The annual flood of the Omo River, so critical for sorghum production, will be eliminated, and water levels in Kenya’s Lake Turkana could be reduced by as much as 22 meters. Major International Financial Institutions (World Bank, European Investment Bank, etc) declined to finance the dam. A significant portion of the funding for the $2 billion project comes from the Industrial-commercial Bank of China.

Ethiopia also has plans to construct Gibe IV and Gibe V dams on the Omo River.

Power generated from the Gibe dams will be used inside Ethiopia or for export to neighboring countries.

Who is Financing

  • Industrial Commercial Bank of China (ICBC)-$500 million financing of Gibe III in August 2010
  • China Development Bank -- $500 million in loans to build two sugar refineries, one of which is in the Omo Valley.
  • Indian Exim Bank- $640 million in financing for Ethiopia’s sugar industry. One of the conditions is that 75% of this loan must be used for goods and services imported from India.
  • World Bank $684 million in financing approved in July 2012 for a transmission line that would export power from Ethiopia to Kenya.

Villagization elsewhere in Ethiopia

The villagization program is part of a nationwide effort to move 1.5 million pastoralists and agro-pastoralists into permanent villages. Human Rights Watch has documented various abuses related to the villagization program in Gambella region . In Gambella, indigenous populations were being moved into new villages by force. Locations were dry and not suitable for food production and services were not being provided as promised. Those who questioned the program were being arbitrarily detained, threatened, or beaten. Due to the negative impacts of this program, several thousand individuals have fled to neighboring South Sudan and Kenya. See Waiting Here for Death for more information Villagization is also underway in Afar, Somali, and Benishangul-Gumuz regions.

Who funds villagization?

The Protection of Basic Services (PBS) program contributes to the salaries of the woreda government employees who are implementing the villagization program. PBS provides services in the areas of education, health care, water, roads and agriculture - the exact five areas of the villagization program. The program pays salaries of teachers, health care extension workers and others who have, in some villages, been asked to mobilize people to move to the new villages. In September 2012 the third round of the PBS program was approved. The major contributors to PBS are the World Bank, DFID, the African Development Bank, and the European Commission.

According to the villagization plan for Lower Omo, the Pastoral Development Community Development Project (PCDP) is also expected to fund certain services in the new village. PCDP is funded by the World Bank and the African Development Bank. The third round of financing for PCDP is to be considered by the World Bank in September 2013.

What others are saying

UN World Heritage Committee: In June 2011, the UN World Heritage Committee called on the Ethiopian government to "immediately halt all construction" on Gibe III, and encouraged the Chinese financiers "to put on hold their financial support" until the Committee’s next annual meeting in June 2012. Neither the Ethiopian government nor ICBC heeded this call.

UN Committee on the Elimination of all Forms of Racial Discrimination: in September 2011 they requested “information on measures taken to conduct an independent assessment of the negative effects of the construction of the [Gibe III and sugar plantation] projects on the livelihood of the Lower Omo peoples and measures taken to consult them in an effective and appropriate manner.” No response has been received.

What is the response of international donors to events in Lower Omo?

Largely silence. The donors have undertaken at least two assessments into events in lower Omo. Transcripts made available from the first assessment reveal that communities shared with DFID/USAID the human rights abuses they had been subjected to. The UK minister for international development, in response to a parliamentary enquiry, stated they were “not able to substantiate the allegations of human rights violation it received during its visit to South Omo in January 2012”. It is not clear what steps have been taken, if any, to substantiate these allegations. No public information has been made available from the second assessment in November 2012.

What is the response of the Government of Kenya?

In August 2011, the Kenyan parliament passed a resolution requiring suspension of dam construction pending further studies. In March 2012 it was announced that the Kenyan government signed an agreement with Ethiopia is purchase up to 400Mw of electricity annually from Ethiopia. The Kenyan government has since been silent on the issues in the Lower Omo Valley that could have a devastating impact on 300,000 Kenyan citizens.
